Xinyang Li

李昕洋

I am a historian of late imperial China, currently pursuing my PhD in History at Cornell University after completing my BA and MA at Wuhan University and Nankai University. My research lies at the intersection of late imperial Chinese history, gender history, the history of the body, and the history of medicine, with a particular interest in how differently gendered bodies perceived, experienced, and articulated illness. I am also the author of Female Historians and Their Historical Works in the Ming and Qing Dynasties (1368–1911) 明清時期的女性史家與史學（1368-1911）, a traditional Chinese monograph published in Taiwan, which reinterprets the intellectual and cultural world of Ming–Qing talented women through the lens of historical writing.

Research

Dissertation

Feeling Virtue: A Cultural History of Gendered Pain in Late Imperial China (1368–1911)

My PhD dissertation examines how pain became a site where gender, morality, and medicine intersected. I analyze four major genres of sources—classical medical theory, case histories and diaries, popular literature on emotional illness, and narratives of practices such as flesh-cutting, footbinding, and wartime violence—to understand how suffering was represented and valued. In these records, women's pain was often narrated as effortless or "painless" when performed out of filial devotion, while men's injuries were described as courageous proof of loyalty and righteousness. By tracing these patterns across medical and non-medical texts, my research shows how pain functioned as a moral discourse that helped uphold late imperial gender hierarchy and notions of virtue.

Life / Interests

I was born in Daqing, Heilongjiang, a young city built on oil, machinery and the steel-hard ideal of production. Its name is woven into modern Chinese memory through slogans like "Learn from Daqing in Industry," the legendary "Iron Man Wang Jinxi," and the ethos known as the "Daqing Spirit." You might expect that such a place would push me toward industrial history or state-led modernity, but the opposite happened. Instead of factories, I became captivated by flesh; instead of pipelines and drill towers, I found myself tracing sensations, injuries and the quiet drama of the human body. Pain, in particular, held me for years like a question I could never finish answering.

My academic curiosity took shape through my own fracture. The day after I broke my thumb, even tasks as ordinary as tying my hair or lifting a cup felt impossibly distant. I searched the regulations for a temporary disability permit and discovered that I did not qualify. It startled me: if I, who could not hold a mug or button a coat, was not "disabled," then who was? Over the following days, my body taught me new technologies of living. The right thumb replaced the left on the keyboard, my grip shifted from circling to supporting, and the unfamiliar slowly became habitual. The pain was inconsistent, too faint to match the textbook fracture, which confused my doctor. For someone unusually tolerant to pain, what does it mean to inhabit a body that hurts, or does not hurt when it should? A painful body demands attention and care; a painless one risks dismissal. Neither is chosen. Sometimes the body simply precedes experience.

These sensations turned into questions that shaped the scholar I am becoming. What counts as illness, and to whom? Who decides what is normal, and where does disability begin? Illness, pain and embodiment are rarely singular physiological facts; they emerge through culture, meaning and practice. As I often remind myself when thinking through Susan Burns's work on leprosy in Japan, the body is a biocultural event: lived, negotiated, interpreted. I return again and again to a line from this week's reading: "You do not have, you are not, a body that hangs together naturally by itself. Keeping yourself whole is one of the tasks of life. It is not given but must be achieved."

Outside of research, I photograph landscapes and everything beyond them, although my favorite subject remains my small dog Yuki. His name means snow, which brings my hometown back to me with every syllable, yet I sometimes call him Yuuki, meaning courage. He is timid, gentle and easily startled, so the name is a wish, a promise that one day he will stand a little braver against the world.
